# Copy Guide Questions

A targeted question bank for translating brand voice into a practical writing guide.
These questions cover what is NOT in the brand brief or website messaging doc — the
contexts writers face, the edge cases, and the practical rules that prevent off-brand copy.

Frameworks: Mailchimp Voice and Tone methodology, Nielsen Norman UX writing principles,
Ann Handley's Content Rules, and plain-language writing standards.

**How to use:** Extract answers already in the brand brief before asking. The copy guide
is mostly synthesis — the interview is short. Focus on contexts, edge cases, and examples.
Ask one question at a time.

---

## 1. Scope & Channels

*Define which surfaces the guide needs to cover — a guide for everything is a guide for nothing.*

- What are the primary channels or surfaces this guide needs to cover? (Website, email campaigns, social media, product UI / in-app copy, ads, support communications, sales collateral — which ones matter most for this brand right now?)
- Of those, which channel is the highest priority — the one where consistency matters most or where mistakes happen most often?
- Are there channels that have meaningfully different audiences or contexts that require distinct tone guidance? (e.g., support emails need a different tone than Instagram)
- Is there a product UI component? (Buttons, error messages, empty states, tooltips, onboarding flows — UX copy has specific rules that differ from marketing copy)

---

## 2. Who Uses the Guide

*A guide written for a solo founder is different from one written for a distributed team of freelancers.*

- Who are the primary users of this guide? (The founder writing everything themselves, an in-house marketing team, freelancers or agencies, AI-assisted copy generation)
- What is their existing familiarity with the brand? (Long-tenured insiders who know the voice intuitively, or new writers who need more explicit instruction?)
- What is their writing background? (Professional copywriters who need brand-specific rules vs. subject-matter experts who need basic writing guidance)
- What format works best for how they'll use it? (Something to read once and internalize, or a reference to look up specific rules mid-project)

---

## 3. Existing Copy: What Works and What Doesn't

*The best copy guides are built from real examples, not abstractions.*

- Is there existing copy — from the website, emails, or social — that perfectly captures the brand voice? Share examples or paste them. These become the "write like this" models.
- Is there existing copy that completely misses the mark — off-brand in tone, too formal, too casual, or just wrong? Share examples. These become the explicit "not this" guidance.
- Are there specific headlines, taglines, or phrases the brand has used that should be treated as canonical — the "how we always talk about this thing"?
- What is the most common mistake or complaint about the brand's current writing? (Too corporate, too casual, too vague, too technical, too salesy — what's the recurring failure mode?)

---

## 4. Edge Cases & Sensitive Contexts

*These are the situations where writers need explicit guidance most urgently.*

- How should copy handle pricing? (Direct and transparent, "let's talk", relative value framing — and what language to use or avoid around cost)
- How should copy handle errors, failures, or problems? (Product errors, delays, complaints — what tone and what to say/not say)
- Are there sensitive topics in this industry where tone must shift? (Health claims, financial outcomes, legal disclaimers, data privacy — where does standard brand voice need to be moderated)
- How should the brand write about competitors? (Name them, avoid naming them, acknowledge the category — what's the stance)
- How should the brand write about itself? ("We believe...", "At [Brand]...", third-person — and when each applies)

---

## 5. Legal & Compliance Constraints

- Are there claims the brand can legally make and should lean into? (Certified, accredited, patented, award-winning, clinically tested — what's verifiable and cleared)
- Are there claims or words that are specifically off-limits for legal or regulatory reasons? (Superlatives like "best" or "only", performance guarantees, health or financial outcome claims)
- Is there an approval process for copy? (Legal review, compliance sign-off, founder approval — and at what stage does it kick in)
- Are there required disclosures or disclaimers that must appear in certain contexts?

---

## 6. Grammar & Style Preferences

*These might seem minor but are where copy guides earn their keep — consistency in the details.*

- Oxford comma: yes or no?
- Numbers: spell out one through nine and use numerals for 10+, or always use numerals, or always spell out?
- Brand name treatment: is there a specific capitalization, styling, or usage rule for the brand name? (e.g., always "iPhone", never "iphone" or "IPHONE")
- Sentence length preference: short and punchy, longer and flowing, or mixed?
- Exclamation points: liberal, sparingly, or never?
- Emoji: never, social only, everywhere, or specific contexts?
- Are there any industry terms that have a specific house spelling or meaning?
