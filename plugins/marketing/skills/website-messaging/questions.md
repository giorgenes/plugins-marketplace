# Website Messaging Questions

A targeted question bank for designing the messaging architecture of a website.
These questions cover what is NOT in the brand brief — conversion goals, site structure,
content inventory, objection handling, and competitive messaging differentiation.

Frameworks: StoryBrand (Donald Miller), MECLABS Conversion Sequence, Nielsen Norman Group
Information Architecture principles, and Jobs-to-be-Done.

**How to use:** Extract answers already in the brand brief before asking. Only ask what remains
unanswered. One question at a time. Push for specifics — "visitors should feel good" is not
a conversion goal.

---

## 1. Business Context & Conversion Goals

*These determine the entire IA and CTA hierarchy of the site.*

- What is the single most important action you want a visitor to take? (The primary conversion — get specific: "book a 30-min demo", "start a free trial", "buy the course", "call the clinic")
- What is the secondary action for visitors who aren't ready for the primary? (newsletter, guide download, watch a video — the "not yet" path)
- Do you have a low-friction entry offer? (free trial, free consultation, free tool, free resource — something that lets visitors experience value before committing)
- What does the brand currently struggle most to communicate online? (If this is a redesign: what do people misunderstand, or what questions does sales keep getting that the site should answer?)
- Is this a new site, a redesign, or a messaging update? If redesign: what specifically isn't working?

---

## 2. Site Structure & Pages

*Establish the information architecture before writing any copy.*

- What pages does the site need? List them. (Homepage, About, Product/Services, Pricing, Blog, Contact, Case studies — what applies?)
- Does the product or service offering need to be broken out? (Multiple products, pricing tiers, service categories — or is it a single offer?)
- Will there be a resources or content hub? (Blog, podcast, guides, help center — or is it a pure conversion site?)
- Are there any pages for specific audiences or use cases? (e.g., "For enterprise", "For small teams", "For [industry]")
- What pages are non-negotiable for legal or compliance reasons? (Privacy policy, terms, cookie notice, regulatory disclaimers)

---

## 3. Audience & Visitor Journey

*Not the same as the brand brief audience — this is about how they arrive and what they need.*

- When someone lands on the homepage, what do they already know? (Referral from someone, saw an ad, searched for a category term, heard the brand name — what's their prior knowledge?)
- Are there meaningfully different audience segments that arrive with different contexts or needs? (B2B decision-maker vs. end-user, new category vs. brand aware, etc.)
- What does a visitor need to believe before they'll take the primary action? (List 2–3 beliefs they must hold — e.g., "this actually works for someone like me", "it's worth the price", "I can trust these people")
- What is the typical journey from first visit to conversion? How many visits? What do they do in between?
- Is there a gatekeeping stakeholder? (Someone who didn't visit the site but will ultimately approve the purchase or sign-off — what do they need to see?)

---

## 4. Content Inventory

*What proof, specifics, and assets exist to support the messaging.*

- What social proof is available? (Testimonials, case studies, named client logos, press mentions, certifications, awards, star ratings — what exists and what's available to use?)
- What numbers anchor credibility? (Customers served, years in business, success rate, time-to-result, money saved, countries, users — any stats that matter)
- What is the specific transformation or result customers achieve? (Before-state and after-state in concrete terms — "went from X to Y in Z time")
- What specific features, capabilities, or services must appear on the homepage vs. deeper pages?
- Is pricing shown publicly or hidden? If hidden: what's the reason, and how should the site handle the "how much does it cost?" question?

---

## 5. Objections & Conversion Barriers

*The copy must preemptively dissolve the barriers between a visitor and the primary action.*

- What are the top 3 reasons people don't convert, even after they've seen the site? (Sales team feedback, customer interviews, support tickets — what's the real hesitation?)
- What is the #1 reason people choose a competitor instead? (If known)
- Is there a trust problem to overcome? (New brand, unusual pricing model, sensitive industry, promise that sounds too good — what makes people skeptical?)
- What are the most common objections the sales team or support hears? ("It's too expensive", "I'm not sure it works for my situation", "I need approval from someone else" — list them)
- What guarantees, return policies, trial terms, or risk-reducers can the brand offer?

---

## 6. Competitive Messaging Context

*Messaging architecture is relative — it's defined partly by what competitors are already saying.*

- Name 2–3 direct competitors. Visit their websites if helpful. What is their homepage headline? How do they lead?
- What messaging do all the competitors share — the category clichés everyone is using? (These are phrases to avoid because they signal sameness)
- What is the one claim this brand can make that no competitor can credibly make? (The owned differentiator — not "better quality" but something specific and verifiable)
- What is the emotional register of the competitive landscape? (Formal and technical? Casual and friendly? Authoritative and serious?) And where does this brand want to sit relative to that?

---

## 7. Voice in Action

*Bridges from the brand brief's voice description to live copy decisions.*

- What are 2–3 words or phrases the brand already uses (or wants to own) that should appear consistently in copy? (Brand vocabulary that is distinct to this brand)
- What are 2–3 words, phrases, or tones that must be avoided — either because competitors use them, they sound wrong for the brand, or they've been banned for other reasons?
- Should copy use "you" freely (speaking directly to the visitor), or is there a more formal register?
- Is humor or wit in scope? If so, what kind — dry, warm, irreverent? And where does it live on the site vs. where should copy stay straight?
- Are there any legal or regulatory constraints on claims? (Can't say "best", "guaranteed", specific health claims, financial performance claims, etc.)

---

## 8. Constraints & Timeline

- When does this need to be live?
- Who will be writing the actual copy? (Founder, internal team, freelancer, agency — this affects how detailed the architecture doc needs to be)
- Are there any existing pages, headlines, or copy blocks that must be preserved? (Legal, contractual, or brand consistency reasons)
- How many languages or locales does the site need to support at launch?
