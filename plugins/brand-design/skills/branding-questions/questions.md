# Brand Discovery Questions

A comprehensive question bank for uncovering brand identity, positioning, and strategy.
These questions draw from established frameworks: Simon Sinek's Golden Circle, Kapferer's Brand Identity Prism,
Jung's 12 Brand Archetypes, the Brand Positioning House, Jobs-to-be-Done theory, and Brand Pyramid methodology.

**Instructions:** Ask questions conversationally. Not all questions apply to every project — use judgment.
Probe incomplete answers with follow-ups. Silence or "I don't know" is valuable signal too.

---

## 1. Purpose & Origin (The WHY)

*Framework: Simon Sinek's Golden Circle — start with WHY, not WHAT.*

- Why does this brand exist beyond making money? What problem in the world does it address?
- What was the founding insight or moment of frustration that sparked this idea?
- If this brand disappeared tomorrow, what would the world lose?
- What is the mission — the change you are trying to make in the world?
- What is the long-term vision — what does success look like in 10 years?
- What beliefs or convictions does the founder(s) hold that drive this brand?
- Is there a manifesto, a rally cry, or a sentence that captures why this brand fights for what it fights for?

---

## 2. The Product / Service (The WHAT & HOW)

- What does this brand actually do, make, or offer? Describe it simply, as if explaining to a curious stranger.
- What is the primary category (e.g. software, fashion, food, consulting, media)?
- What is the core feature or activity that everything else revolves around?
- What does the experience of using this product/service feel like, step by step?
- What does the brand do that no one else does — the secret ingredient or proprietary method?
- What makes delivery or execution distinctive (faster, more personal, more rigorous, more accessible)?

---

## 3. Target Audience & Personas

*Framework: Jobs-to-be-Done — people hire brands to get a job done, functional and emotional.*

- Who is the primary audience? Be specific: age range, occupation, income, geography, lifestyle stage.
- If you had to describe your ideal customer in one sentence (role + aspiration + context), what would it be?
- What is this person trying to accomplish — their functional job-to-be-done?
- What emotional job are they hiring this brand to do (feel confident, feel safe, feel belonging, feel cool)?
- What social job — how does using this brand change how others perceive them?
- What does this person fear most in this context? What keeps them up at night?
- What does this person deeply aspire to? What does success look like for them?
- What does a day in their life look like when they encounter this brand?
- Who is the secondary audience (influencers, decision-makers, buyers who differ from users)?
- Who is explicitly NOT the target audience — and why is that deliberate?

---

## 4. Competitive Landscape & Positioning

*Framework: Brand Positioning Statement — "For [audience] who [need], [brand] is [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [alternatives], [brand] [key differentiator]."*

- Name 3–5 direct competitors. How does each make the audience feel? What do they stand for?
- Name 2–3 indirect competitors or substitute behaviors (what people do instead of using this brand).
- On what dimensions does this brand want to win (price, quality, speed, depth, community, design, ethics)?
- On what dimensions is this brand willing to lose or not compete?
- Where is the white space — the position no competitor currently occupies that this brand can own?
- What do competitors do well that this brand respects but won't copy?
- What do competitors do that frustrates customers, and how does this brand solve that frustration?
- In one sentence: what is the brand's positioning statement?

---

## 5. Brand Personality & Archetype

*Framework: Jung's 12 Brand Archetypes — each archetype speaks to a universal human motivation.*

**The 12 Archetypes (use as a mirror):**
- **Innocent** — optimistic, simple, honest (Dove, Coca-Cola)
- **Explorer** — free-spirited, adventurous, authentic (Jeep, Patagonia)
- **Sage** — knowledgeable, trustworthy, analytical (Google, BBC)
- **Hero** — courageous, determined, inspiring (Nike, FedEx)
- **Outlaw** — rebellious, disruptive, liberating (Harley-Davidson, Oatly)
- **Magician** — visionary, transformative, charismatic (Apple, Disney)
- **Regular Guy/Gal** — down-to-earth, unpretentious, relatable (IKEA, eBay)
- **Lover** — passionate, intimate, sensual (Victoria's Secret, Häagen-Dazs)
- **Jester** — playful, funny, spontaneous (Mailchimp, Dollar Shave Club)
- **Caregiver** — nurturing, protective, empathetic (Johnson & Johnson, Volvo)
- **Creator** — imaginative, original, inventive (Lego, Adobe)
- **Ruler** — authoritative, organized, prestigious (Rolex, McKinsey)

Questions:
- If this brand were a person at a party, how would they behave? What would they wear? What would they talk about?
- Which of the 12 archetypes feels most true? Which feels most aspirational? Is there tension between them?
- Pick 5 adjectives that describe this brand's personality. Now remove 2 that feel like hygiene (true of all competitors). What's left?
- Pick 3 adjectives that this brand is explicitly NOT. What would be out of character?
- If this brand were an animal, a car, a celebrity, a film genre — what would it be and why?
- What is the brand's relationship with the audience: a mentor, a peer, a challenger, a servant, a friend?

---

## 6. Brand Values & Promise

*Framework: Brand Pyramid — Features → Functional Benefits → Emotional Benefits → Values → Essence.*

- List 3–5 core values. For each: what does this brand actually DO that proves this value, beyond claiming it?
- What is the brand promise — the single thing this brand commits to delivering every time, reliably?
- What is the brand essence — one word or short phrase that captures the soul of this brand?
- Which values are held privately (guide decisions internally) vs. proclaimed publicly (used in communication)?
- What would the brand refuse to do, even if it were profitable, because it would violate its values?
- How does this brand want people to feel after every interaction — the emotional aftertaste?

---

## 7. Visual Identity Direction

*These are directional, not prescriptive — the goal is to surface intent, not design.*

**Color**
- Are there any colors that feel instinctively right? What do they say?
- Are there colors to avoid, and why (competitor associations, cultural concerns, wrong emotional register)?
- Should the palette feel warm or cool, saturated or muted, bold or restrained?
- Reference 2–3 brands whose color palette you admire. What do they have in common?

**Typography**
- Should the wordmark feel modern or classic, geometric or humanist, structured or expressive?
- Is legibility at small sizes critical (tech product) or is expressiveness at large sizes more important (fashion, culture)?
- Serif, sans-serif, or display? Why?

**Imagery & Style**
- What kind of photography or illustration feels right — documentary, polished, editorial, flat, textured, lo-fi?
- Should visuals feel like they were made by humans (handmade, imperfect) or crafted by machines (precise, clean)?
- Reference 3 brands or campaigns whose visual world resonates. What specifically works?
- What visual styles or aesthetics are off-limits?

**Overall Feel**
- Dense or airy? Loud or quiet? Maximalist or minimalist?
- Should the brand feel like it belongs to a specific decade, culture, or subculture?
- When someone sees a brand asset, what is the first emotion they should feel?

---

## 8. Brand Voice & Tone

*Framework: Voice is constant; tone shifts by context. Define voice first.*

- If this brand wrote a tweet, what would it sound like? Write a sample.
- How does the brand talk about itself — first person ("we"), second person ("you"), third person?
- Where does it sit on these axes:
  - Formal ←→ Casual
  - Serious ←→ Playful
  - Expert ←→ Accessible
  - Direct ←→ Warm
  - Minimal ←→ Expressive
- What vocabulary or phrases does the brand own — words it uses that others don't?
- What vocabulary is banned — words or tones that feel off-brand?
- How does the tone shift between a marketing headline, a product UI label, an error message, and a customer complaint response?
- Name a brand (inside or outside the category) whose voice you admire. What specifically works?

---

## 9. Brand Story & Narrative

*Framework: Story structure — Situation → Conflict → Resolution → Transformation.*

- What is the origin story in 3 sentences: what was wrong in the world, what the founder did about it, what changed as a result?
- Who is the hero of the brand story — the brand itself, the founder, or the customer?
- What is the conflict or villain in the brand story (a broken system, an outdated belief, a lazy industry)?
- What is the transformation the brand delivers — before-state vs. after-state for the customer?
- Does the brand have any rituals, mythology, or founding lore that insiders know and love?
- What is the brand's relationship with culture: does it lead culture, reflect culture, or deliberately resist culture?

---

## 10. Brand Experience & Touchpoints

*Framework: Kapferer's Brand Identity Prism — Physique, Personality, Culture, Relationship, Reflection, Self-image.*

- Where does the customer first encounter this brand? What is that moment like?
- What are the 3–5 most important touchpoints (website, packaging, onboarding, support, social)?
- At each key touchpoint, what is the expected experience? Where does the brand currently fail?
- What is the one brand touchpoint that, if done perfectly, would define the brand in memory?
- How should the offline and online experience feel — seamlessly unified, or are there legitimate differences?
- What is the "moment of delight" — the unexpected thing the brand does that makes people tell others about it?

---

## 11. Audience Self-Perception (Reflection & Self-Image)

*Framework: Kapferer — "Reflection" is who the brand projects; "Self-image" is how users see themselves when using it.*

- When someone uses or wears or recommends this brand, what does it say about them to others? (Reflection)
- How does using this brand make the customer feel about themselves internally? (Self-image)
- Is this a brand people show off publicly or keep as a personal preference?
- What tribe or identity does this brand give access to?
- What does choosing this brand say about a person's values, taste, or worldview?

---

## 12. Cultural & Market Context

- Is this brand global, regional, or hyper-local? Should it feel of a specific place?
- Are there cultural sensitivities, taboos, or regional meanings to consider for colors, names, symbols, or gestures?
- Is this brand entering an established category (needs clear differentiation) or creating a new category (needs education)?
- Is the broader cultural moment working for or against this brand? What tailwinds exist?
- What cultural movements, subcultures, or communities does this brand belong to or want to belong to?

---

## 13. Constraints & Boundaries

*The "what not to do" is as valuable as the "what to do."*

- What is the budget range for brand identity work (logo only, full identity system, brand guidelines + launch)?
- What is the timeline? Is there a hard deadline (launch event, funding announcement, product release)?
- Are there existing assets that must be preserved (logo, color, name, tagline)?
- Are there trademark or legal constraints on name, logo, or category claims?
- Are there technical constraints (must work in one color, must be readable at 16px, must print on kraft paper)?
- What has been tried before that failed? Why did it fail?
- Who has final approval on brand decisions? How many stakeholders?

---

## 14. Success & Measurement

- How will you know the brand is working? What does success look like in 6 months, 2 years?
- What do you want people to say about this brand when asked to describe it to a friend?
- What do you want people to feel the first time they encounter this brand?
- What metric or signal would tell you the brand has failed?
- Is there a brand you aspire to be compared to in 5 years?

---

## 15. The Stress-Test Questions

*Ask these when answers feel too safe, generic, or polished.*

- "If this brand had a sworn enemy — a belief or behavior it actively fights against — what would it be?"
- "What is the most controversial thing this brand actually believes that competitors won't say out loud?"
- "If you could only keep one thing about this brand — one quality, one promise, one feeling — what would it be and why?"
- "What would a journalist write a critical profile about this brand? What's the uncomfortable truth?"
- "If you removed the logo and swapped in a competitor's logo, what would look identical? That's not your brand. What's left?"
- "In three years, what do you hope people never say about this brand?"
- "What is the thing about this brand that would make 50% of people love it and 50% of people not care? (Generic brands appeal to everyone and mean nothing.)"
